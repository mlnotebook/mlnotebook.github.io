    • add PhD Medical Image Computing

      Imperial College, London

      Dates: October 2016 - September 2019
      Award: Doctor of Philosophy in Medical Image Computing

      My PhD is centered around medical image analysis in large-scale imaging studies. I am routinely using thousands of images, often from the UK Biobank imaging study, to drive machine learning algorithms; particularly employing deep learning.

      In the first year, I primarily focused on automated quality control of cardiac MRI segmentations in large-scale databases. This research was presented at the MICCAI 2017 conference in Quebec City, Canada and is due to be published in Lecture Notes in Computer Science.

      The remainder of my research is directed towards constructing generative adversarial networks (GANs) which are able to take phenotype inputs from a population imaging database to inform their outputs.

      Award: Master of Research in Medical Imaging Sciences - Distinction

      This degree represents the first of four years on the EPSRC Centre for Doctoral Training (CDT) in Medical Imaging jointly hosted between King's College London and Imperial College London. I am within the second cohort to attend the CDT based at St. Thomas' Hospital.

      The MRes programme is structured such that students gain a solid foundation in medical imaging sciences before specialising into one of three streams. I chose to advance in Stream Three: Medical Image Computing and Computational Modelling. I've studies modules in medical image reconstruction, medical image computing, computational modelling and machine learning.

      We undertook a group project in the radiation labs at St. Thomas' entitled: Screening a Peptide Array for Radiolabeling Efficiency Using Imaging Methods. Both this and my individual MRes project, Automated Quality Control for Cardiac MRI Segmentations" led to an overal award of distinction for this degree.

    • add 2017/09 - "Automatic Quality Control of Cardiac MRI Segmentation in Large-scale Population Imaging"

      Journal: Lecture Notes in Computer Science Vol. 10433
      Authors: Robinson, R., Valindra, V., Bai, W., Suzuki, H., Matthews, P. M., Page, C., Rueckert, D., & Glocker, B.

      Abstract: The trend towards large-scale studies including population imaging poses new challenges in terms of quality control (QC). This is a particular issue when automatic processing tools such as image segmentation methods are employed to derive quantitative measures or biomarkers for further analyses. Manual inspection and visual QC of each segmentation result is not feasible at large scale. However, it is important to be able to detect when an automatic method fails to avoid inclusion of wrong measurements into subsequent analyses which could otherwise lead to incorrect conclusions. To overcome this challenge, we explore an approach for predicting segmentation quality based on reverse classification accuracy, which enables us to discriminate between successful and failed cases. We validate this approach on a large cohort of cardiac MRI for which manual QC scores were available. Our results on 7,425 cases demonstrate the potential for fully automatic QC in the context of large-scale population imaging such as the UK Biobank Imaging Study

      Citation:

      Robinson, R., Valindra, V., Bai, W., Suzuki, H., Matthews, P. M., Page, C., Rueckert, D., & Glocker, B. (2017). Automatic Quality Control of Cardiac MRI Segmentation in Large-scale Population Imaging. In Medical Image Computing and Computer-Assisted Intervention – MICCAI 2017 (pp. tbc). September 2017.

    • add 2009/09 - "Electrophysiological substrate for a dominant reentrant source during atrial fibrillation"

      Journal: Engineering in Medicine and Biology Society, 2009. Annual International Conference of the IEEE
      Authors: Aslanidi, O. V., Robinson, R., Cheverton, D., Boyett, M. R., & Henggui Zhang.
      DOI: https://doi.org/10.1109/IEMBS.2009.5333573

      Abstract: Experimentally observed differences in the action potential (AP) properties between the left (LA) and right (RA) atria are believed to be important in maintaining reentrant sources during atrial fibrillation. We incorporate AP models for single LA and RA cells, as well as major intra- and interatrial conduction pathways, into a 2D atrial tissue model and study the role of tissue heterogeneity in global interactions between reentrant spiral waves in both atria. Our simulations show that shorter AP refractoriness in the LA translates into a shorter period of spiral rotation, and as a result, reentry in the LA dominates the overall excitation patterns in the atria.

      Citation:

      Aslanidi, O. V., Robinson, R., Cheverton, D., Boyett, M. R., & Henggui Zhang. (2009). Electrophysiological Substrate for a Dominant Reentrant Source during Atrial Fibrillation. In 2009 Annual International Conference of the IEEE Engineering in Medicine and Biology Society (pp. 2819–2822). IEEE. https://doi.org/10.1109/IEMBS.2009.5333573

    • add 2017/07 - Royal Society Summer Science

      Event: Summer Science Exhibition 2017
      Capacity: Exhibitor
      Location: The Royal Society, London


      The Royal Society Summer Science (RSSS) exhibition takes parts in the grounds of the Royal Society, in London. It is one of the most well-known and well-respected public engagement events in the scientific community. Exhibitors from across the country, from academic institutions and industry, demonstrate their cutting-edge research to the public across 7 days. It is an opportunity to engage people of all ages with Science.

      This year our stand was all about the heart. 3D-printed models of healthy and diseased hearts were shown alongside their corresponding MRI scans. A gyroscopic 3D model demonstrated resynchronisation therapy. The star of the show was "Heart in Your Hands".
